### 2. Pacing & Action

Line#3: "Humidity clings to his skin." is unfilmable. A camera cannot capture the feeling of humidity. Show him physically sweating or wiping his brow to ground the atmosphere.

Line#4: "Tight chest." is an internal physical state. An actor can play this, but it is better written as a physical action, such as "Benjamin clutches his chest, gasping for air."

Line#5: "like it is coming through water." is an auditory description written as an active physical action. It should be formatted as a sound design note or parenthetical, not as a visual action line.

Line#14: "The officer's voice stays calm, trained." describes an auditory quality and internal state inside an action block. Keep action blocks strictly visual.

Line#23: "Grit and panic claw at his throat." is overly novelistic and internal. Show his physical struggle—choking, coughing, clawing at his neck—rather than using literary metaphors.

Line#34: "smiling like they know him." is an unfilmable direction. Describe the facial expression objectively rather than assigning an untranslatable intent to the smile.

Line#36: "luminous and calm, unsettlingly still." uses abstract adjectives to describe a character's vibe. Describe her physical behavior (e.g., "she stands without blinking, arms perfectly rigid at her sides") to show this stillness.

Line#56: "A friendly smile that does not quite reach her eyes." is a literary cliché. It is incredibly difficult for an actor to register this clearly on camera without a massive, distracting close-up.

Line#92: "As they walk down the hallway." is an incomplete action fragment that halts the flow of the script.

Line#134: "He stares down the room like he owns it. Or wants to burn it down." is a novelistic interpretation of Jace's internal state. Stick to what we can see.

Line#143: "Like a séance, not a session." is a literary comparison. Describe the visual setup of the room (e.g., "candles guttering on low tables") instead of using a simile.

Line#157: "Not unkind. Not comforting either." is an internal director's note. Show her neutral, unreadable expression through her physical stillness.

Line#259: "walls begin to breathe... like the room itself is sighing." is purple prose. Describe the physical visual effect (e.g., "the plaster on the walls warps inward and outward") to help the VFX team.

Line#281: "The air feels thick, pressing against his skin." describes an unfilmable tactile sensation. Show Benjamin physically reacting to the pressure change instead.

Line#360: "The air feels like it's holding its breath." is an unfilmable, literary abstraction that slows down the script's pacing.

Line#394: "She says it like she heard it whispered." is a director's note telling the actor how to deliver a line using a simile. Let the actress interpret the subtext.

Line#419: "Walls feel like they're pulsing." uses passive, weak language ("feel like"). Keep it active and punchy: "The walls pulse."

Line#459: "feels human" is an abstract, non-visual description. Detail the specific warm, human elements of the room instead of telling us how it feels.

Line#608: "moves like she's following choreography only she can hear." is an unfilmable comparison. Describe the physical, rhythmic manner of her walk instead.

Line#1063: "Eyes unreadable." is an unfilmable and overused shortcut. Describe the blankness or lack of expression instead.

Line#1119: "unnervingly serene" is an abstract director's note. Show her serenity through her slow, silent movements.

Line#1155: "The room holds its breath." is a novelistic description that cannot be translated visually to the screen.

Line#1336: "silent but wide-eyed." is static facial direction. Action lines should drive movement and progression.

Line#1720: "Her sleep is shallow... like she is reacting to something unseen." is too vague and internal. Describe her tossing and turning, or her eyelids fluttering.

Line#1721: "like fingers searching." is a simile that slows down the visual rhythm of the action block. Just state: "Shadows crawl across the floor."

Line#1910: "The shadows don't move — they watch." is a logical contradiction that is impossible to film. Shadows cannot "watch" without eyes or movement.

Line#1919: "A strange peaceful unease fills the room." is a highly abstract, contradictory emotional description that cannot be filmed.

Line#2049: "unreadable." is used again as a placeholder for actual facial description.

Line#2228: "something is... off" is an unfilmable placeholder. Cut this line and go straight to the excellent visual descriptions of her jewelry and bracelet that follow.

Line#2304: "Her voice lands wrong. Too light. Too practiced. Like she's performing." belongs in parentheticals or should be shown through her physical performance, not written as block action.

Line#2812: "The camera pans wide." is a technical camera direction. Spec scripts should avoid camera directions to keep the read smooth and respect the director's role.

Line#2813: "CLOSE-UP: a patient..." is another unnecessary technical camera direction. Simply describe the patient's face to naturally imply the close-up.

Line#3063: "almost imperceptibly." is a self-defeating word in screenwriting. If an action is imperceptible, it cannot be seen by the audience.

Line#3118: "The camera holds too long." is a direct camera and editing instruction. Omit this to keep the focus on the story elements.

Line#3221: "loaded, brimming with things unsaid." is a purely literary description of an internal atmosphere. Show this tension through their physical posture and distance.


### 3. Formatting & Clarity

Line#22: "INT. HOSPITAL ROOM - UNKNOWN TIME" uses "UNKNOWN TIME" as a time-of-day indicator. Screenplays must use standard indicators like "DAY" or "NIGHT" to assist the production and lighting crew.

Line#36: "DR. ANNA (30s)" is introduced here, but throughout the script her character name in headings switches inconsistently between "ANNA", "DR. ANNA", and "DOCTOR". Standardize her name to "DR. ANNA" across all dialogue blocks.

Line#52-53: "He passes a wall of clocks, none ticking. One runs backward. He squints — one clock runs backward." contains a glaring repetition. Line#53 repeats the exact same clock detail from Line#52. Cut one of these lines.

Line#95: "INT. CAFETERIA – LATER" uses a different dash symbol (en-dash) compared to the hyphens used in other scene headings. Standardize your scene heading punctuation throughout the script.

Line#123: "CARMILLA (O.S.)" speaks before she is introduced on Line#125. Characters must be introduced in the action lines before they speak, or immediately as they speak.

Line#183: "DOCTOR" is used as the character name here, adding to the naming inconsistency of Dr. Anna.

Line#241: "CONT'D — INT. AMBER'S ROOM – NIGHT" is formatted incorrectly. "CONT'D" belongs in dialogue blocks, not in scene headings. Use "CONTINUOUS" if the scene flows directly from the previous one, or simply use a fresh scene heading.

Line#376: "EXTENSION — INT. BENJAMIN'S ROOM – NIGHT" uses "EXTENSION," which is not a standard or recognized screenwriting term. Use a standard scene heading.

Line#503: "BENJAMIN enters to find OTTO..." describes Otto's coat and medals in detail, even though Otto was already introduced on Line#99. Avoid re-introducing established characters with descriptive blocks.

Line#510: "A SMALL FIGURE..." is introduced but not named, only to be casually revealed as "MALACHAI" on Line#579. Introduce Malachai with his name, age, and physical description the moment he appears on screen.

Line#528: "AMBER (O.S.)" speaks before she physically enters the scene. Her entry block on Line#530 should not re-introduce her with capitalized lettering since she was already introduced on Line#182.

Line#578: "Cut to Malachai sitting in the floor." is formatted poorly. "Cut to" is an outdated transition that shouldn't start an action line. Additionally, "sitting in the floor" is a typo and should be "sitting on the floor."

Line#604: "CUT TO: Amber clutching her cat, unsettled." embeds a transition ("CUT TO:") directly into an action line. Transitions should be placed on the far right margin, or omitted entirely for modern pacing.
